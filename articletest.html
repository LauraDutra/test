DAHLIA (she/they)

Dahlia is a musician, poet, and creative from the Bronx now based in Berkeley.
I was sitting on my New York bed when Dahlia joined the call from Berkeley, where she’d been staying at an LGBTQ+ co-op. I saw the corners of their room, the wooden desk they sat at, and was hit with a wave of familiarity—this was the same co-op I had stayed at nine months ago when I was seeking temporary housing as a displaced person. I thought to myself about how care works in cycles, how our projects of caring for one another and for ourselves always remain incomplete, continually sustaining us.
 
My face lit up as soon as I saw her, with Dahlia radiant as always. I wished I could’ve seen her in person, but nonetheless her gentle enthusiasm reached me across the screen and enveloped us in a digital coast-to-coast embrace.
 
I asked Dahlia to introduce herself, ready to learn more about her journey of finding community care: where it’s taken her, where she’s going, and what she’s sitting with currently.

Aristotle X:
Hello, Dahlia. Why don't we start with just an introduction of yourself, your work, where you're based, where you are now?
 
Dahlia Bekong:
My name is Dahlia. I'm a 19-year-old Black transfemme with ancestral ties to Cameroon, in West Africa. I would consider myself under the title of a creative because I tend to feel like being in and out of different disciplines. But right now, I'm mostly focused on poetry and digital music production. I am originally from the Bronx and raised in New York City. Because I'm in a transition state right now, I am staying in the Bay Area.
 
X:
That sounds like such a big cultural shift. One thing that I'm wondering about is when you're talking about care and nourishment in your work, what are you working on right now in the Bay Area that is informed by this care? What are you drawing inspiration from for your work?
 
Bekong:
One of the things that I am so happy to see in the Bay Area is just, to a certain degree, queerness just out and about, and in a way that is focused on the beauty of queerness and not necessarily the power of queerness.
 
And I think the differentiation between beauty and power is something that I'm realizing more and more as I understand how queerness within the modern age is packaged and marketed as a cultural commodity. Comparing that to New York again, I feel like you can be as weird as you want in New York. I mean, you can basically be anything you want within New York as long as you are able to profit from that.
 
And I've recognized that from a very young age—the people who were the most prevalent within New York were the people able to make money. And within that barrier, that's inspired me to be a lot more in touch with not necessarily having to focus on the profitability of my work, but more so on how that work is able to speak into a lot of my identity and experiences as someone who suffers a lot from mental health issues, who experiences a lot of isolation due to both my unique experiences from my identity and also through homelessness.
I’d only been in New York a few months at that point, and I’d only seen a fraction of what Dahlia had seen, but their response made me reflect on my own experiences in the city. Listening to Dahlia, I reminded myself to actively seek out moments and spaces in New York that resisted the endless grind of labor and profit that they’d described.
 
There were moments where I’d found it—anonymous graffiti that turned subway adverts into collages, queer art installations made from discarded plants and decorations, the mysterious trail of birdseed that appeared every morning along the railing of my local library without fail.
 
I also considered the sharp contrast between Dahlia’s experiences and the version of New York that gets packaged and sold to us on billboards and storefronts: how hundred-story skyscrapers can exist within the same system that turns homelessness into a death sentence, how someone can feel so isolated in the biggest city in the United States. These are precarious realities many of us live through on a daily basis, and I kept this in mind while listening to Dahlia describe how her art is influenced by her lived experiences.
Bekong: (cont.)
Right now I’m working on an instrumental/rap project that is focusing on depicting how childhood trauma can inform the way that we move within the world and the ways that we sort of expect ourselves to relate to other people, and what happens when we’re able to distance ourselves from this world and be willing to just take the next step of creating entire new places that our inner child has always wanted.
 
It's always focusing on “What would I have wanted when I was a kid?” and not, like, materialistic stuff. Although, you know, I like getting myself random candy every once in a while to just nourish myself.

But I think there is a very big inspiration here to just go back to your roots and not be ashamed of the things that other people see as immature or not respectable. I think a huge part of it is the rejection of respectability politics and just being who you are, unabashedly.

"There is a very big inspiration here to just go back to your roots and not be ashamed of the things that other people see as immature or not respectable . . . a huge part of it is the rejection of respectability politics."
​ ​X:
I'm thinking about respectability politics right now for you and your work, and I'm thinking about how that interacts with being able to nourish your inner child. What are some ways that you have been nourishing your inner child, whether creatively, or physically, or spiritually in your time here so far?
 
Bekong:
Right now, physically, I actually have—like, here—some Trolli peach rings and some sweet tea. And this is gonna sound a little bit silly, but candy is actually a huge way that I feel like I've been nourishing myself, nourishing my inner child, like, physically. I think a lot of adults sort of joke about like, oh, they're able to do all the stuff that they wanted to do as a kid or buy all the stuff they wanted to as a kid. And I think a part of me takes that literally where every time I literally consume a physical piece of candy, there is this inner elation. Don’t get me wrong: it's not like I never ate candy as a kid, but this inner elation is something that grounds me. This sugar in this peach ring grounds me on a very emotional level.
Here Dahlia held a bag of peach rings and a can of sweet tea up to the camera. These, I thought, are the ways we care for ourselves—returning to memory and recognizing what grounds us. I admired Dahlia’s reasoning behind her processes of care. She was incredibly aware of what she needed, not just to survive but to heal and grow in new environments. I reflected how traveling from coast to coast can be a sudden upheaval in itself, and so continued listening, curious about what memories Dahlia had brought to Berkeley.
​Bekong: (cont.)
In terms of spirituality, I’ve been getting a lot more into music that sort of reminds me of my childhood, so I’ve been re-exploring a lot of gospel music and—by extension—neo-soul. I've actually been listening to a lot of Lauren Hill, Jill Scott, D’Angelo, Maxwell, artists that basically encapsulated the pinnacle of those root genres I was exposed to, which were mostly gospel and like a little bit of influence from 70’s and 80’s pop.
 
As an extension from that, I’ve also been trying to get more into the production of Afrobeats. The way I'm trying to execute that is by sampling a lot of the Makossa or traditional West African rhythms and combining that with drums, the sort of stuff they use in trap music. I think that sampling West African grooves is necessarily like recreating them, as a lot of contemporary Afrobeats artists do, so it allows me to hone in on my inspirations from why I'm actually doing this, why I'm trying to create these instrumentals.
 
And that's because I want to take something from my childhood that represented my ancestral roots and reconfigure that into a form that sort of acknowledges that, “Yeah, I am from here,” but at the same time there has been so much other stuff that's influenced me that I don't think just simply saying that I have ancestral ties to Cameroon would ever really capture or describe [it].
Trans ancestry is difficult, especially for racialized trans people—many of us no longer speak to our families, or don’t have a traceable record. The archives of history don’t have the answers that we are looking for, often because these keys to our past are withheld by centuries of violence spanning systems of racial capitalism, settler colonialism, and imperialism.

At the same time, these systems have withheld much of the connection and care that we seek with our queer and trans identities—ways of being that go beyond binaries, different identities and orientations. Dahlia personally works against these reductionist readings of history and told me how their work has helped them redefine West African identity for themselves.
​
X:
I'm thinking about what you're saying in regards to using or being influenced by your ancestral roots. And I think especially for diasporic trans people of color, there is, like you had said, a lot of trauma surrounding the inner child. So many of us grow up so much faster than we need to. That's one thing I'm wondering about—in what ways has being a trans person of color influenced or affected the ways that your ancestry connects with your music or is influenced by your music?
 
Bekong:
I think that is a kind of question that I'm trying to figure out myself, to be quite honest. That whole method of sampling is something that I was still, on a methodological level, trying to figure out how to integrate. I think as a diasporic transfemme person, I'm trying to figure out how much of my ancestry should go into my work. And I think this goes back into how my work deals with a lot of my childhood experiences. Even before I came out, I was very queer-coded as a child. There was always something a bit off about me to my extended family and to my immediate family.
 
That sort of caused me to have this little bit of a love-hate relationship with my ancestry for the longest time. And even now there is like a part of me that's like, “I don't need it. It's not been useful to me.”
 
At the same time, there's also a part of me that’s like, “Well, the version that your family showed you does not represent your true ancestry.” And I think, first and foremost, I want to focus on having my work redefine what it means to be African, what it means to be West African. And I think this idea that there is one way to be traditional or to pay homage to your indigenous roots is something that's irking me so much, especially as I uncover more and more about the queer histories of West Africa.
 
And it's such a strange thing, because as a child, I was always raised very heavily Catholic. And my parents have always told me that something like being straight and being cisgender is the traditional way of doing things.
 
But if you look at it [through] a historical lens, that is absolutely not true. That is definitely not true. There have been so many ways of being on a gender-based and sexuality-based level, I don't even think it's possible to capture it by saying “gay” or “bisexual,” using those labels. I would say maybe “queer” is the closest to it, but even queer doesn't encapsulate that, because queer implies that it is a shifting from what is considered normal, what is considered the dominant culture.
​
​"​I think this idea that there is one way to be traditional or to pay homage to your indigenous roots is something that's irking me so much, especially as I uncover more and more about the queer histories of West Africa."
​
Bekong: (cont.)
But when you focus on West Africa pre-colonization, it is just simply just a different form of ontology that you're dealing with that “queerness” doesn't begin to encapsulate because of both the fact that it was not “queer”—it was not different—and also the fact that in terms of sexuality, that act of having someone based off of their gender was not—there was nothing to be queer for.
 
Like it wasn't that you had sex with the same gender or sex with the different gender, because the way that your sexuality was encapsulated was never really tied to that. It was always tied to political utility.
 
So like for example, I don't remember, but there was this West African group that we were studying when I was still at Binghamton: a lot of the ways they sort of like engaged in family-making was through the ability for a woman within the family—or, well, they’d be considered a woman, but someone who was able to reproduce—to be able to have children.
 
So the womb was the way that families were created. And so you have cis woman/cis man couples coming together. But then a woman would marry another woman and that wouldn't be considered queer because there's nothing queer about it. It's literally just expanding the family. It's a political project.
 
And so that has opened my eyes because I'm realizing that there are entirely different modes of thinking that I need to just uncover. It's literally digging through the archives because everything I was taught as a child that was seen as traditional or indigenous to West Africa was really just a part of colonization. And so there's a deliberate effort within my art to try to expose what I uncovered in that research.
 
X:
When I'm thinking about these next few questions about the future or about looking forward, one question I have—building off of what you're saying here—is when you're saying that you have lived and been the margins or within the margins of so many different identities, how do you—or do you choose to— envision a trans future for yourself? And more importantly, for you what does that concept of a trans future look like?
 
Bekong:
I've been thinking about that a lot. And I feel like if you asked me that a month ago—I think my honest, honest-to-God answer is that I do not see a future for myself and it's just sort of a very depressing reality.
 
But I think when I literally think about the future, I don't see anything. ’Cause I don't know where I'm going. I don't know, like—I know what would sort of like need to happen for me to feel okay. But that would require such a rupturing of the fabrics of how this world works that would, you know, be completely unrecognizable to anyone who's currently existing now.
 
But I think one thing that I want the future to be is just a place where I don't have to worry about surviving. I think a lot of the time I live day in and day out worrying about what tomorrow will bring, what a week will bring, what the next month will bring.
 
I think when I look into a month into the future, I literally cannot envision myself existing within a month because I know that the way that I'm existing now, my conditions are so fragile that even one push in the wrong direction will cause everything to crumble. And I mean that on both a physical and emotional level. I feel like if I had the ability to just survive, like if I could just sit down and focus on expressing myself or engaging in the crafts that allow me to—like—make me happy, then I think that's what my ideal future would be.

That's what I feel like a future that would be beneficial to trans people would be—the ability to sit down and explore themselves and to not have to worry about the way that the material world will sort of hold their survival as a bargaining chip against them.

I thought again about precarity. I wondered how different Dahlia’s life and work might be if she did not have to think about surviving to the next day, week, or month. I thought about what the world would be like if survival was not so fragile for our community.
 
Having previously listened to Dahlia’s music, I sat in silence for a moment, recalling the contrast between her instrumentals, which evoke a haunted whimsy, and the limits Dahlia lives within in terms of envisioning a future. With this added context, their music took on new meaning for me: it became a reflection of producing beauty against the stark, everyday material violences of the world. Performance is a survival strategy for many of us—the very act of self-expression is a way we transform echoes of struggle and imbue them with our visions of other, better worlds worth fighting for.

X:
What advice—or maybe what words—would you have for other trans youth who might one day be listening to this interview or reading about it, especially Black trans youth or displaced trans youth who have gone through experiences or occupy identities similar to yourself? What words do you have for them?
​
​​Bekong:
I think I have a couple pieces of advice. I think the first would be—and I'm gonna say this in two forms so it doesn't sound as harsh, but also I feel the duality of the forms in saying this:
 
The first form is that you are your biggest advocate. And the second form is don't expect sh-t from anyone. And I mean to say that people will help you, but at the same time you need to learn how to reach out to people, to make it clear what you need. Because more so than not, people have an idea about what your level of need is, but the needs of Black trans youths—from financial to emotional, to just opportunities to be able to engage in activities that they want—are so, so underestimated because we are not seen as deserving of care.
 
I think literally on a physical level in the medical industry, doctors think that Black women can endure a magnitude more pain than their white counterparts. And that sort of shows that even in what would be seen as the most, like, scientific level of measuring needs, that Black people's needs are severely underestimated.
 
So making it clear, giving yourself in developing the ability to be able to sort of ask for those things and making your needs known is gonna be very crucial.

"That's what I feel like a future that would be beneficial to trans people would be — the ability to sit down and explore themselves and to not have to worry about the way that the material world will sort of hold their survival as a bargaining chip against them."
​
Bekong: (cont.)
I think the second is—this is gonna sound really cliché—but just, like, be yourself. I sound like an English teacher, but I think everything within a Western field of ontology—and that means the way that everything post-colonialism has taught us about how we know and categorize and comprehend things—sort of forces us to box things into certain categories and make that packaged.
 
And I think that ties a lot into how we're packaging things to make things palatable to a lot of consumers or other groups. And I think to a certain degree you are gonna need to do that because you gotta get money. But also make a clean separation between your engaging in that as a form of survival and having the core of yourself that will never be restricted to you. Knowing who you are.
 
If you don't know, discover that: start exploring that as soon as possible, but you don't need to do that out loud. You don't need to be like, “I'm gonna out myself immediately,” because, please, survival first. But do the intellectual work, the emotional work of figuring out who you are.
 
I think the third would also probably be to make community, please find support and resources. I literally would not be here if it wasn't for the people who cared for me.

​Dahlia’s newest release, silent hymns from a food desert, an instrumental project exploring their time in Binghamton, NY, is available on all platforms. We encourage you to donate to Dahlia Bekong’s GoFundMe and explore her full portfolio.
